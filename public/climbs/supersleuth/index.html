VII, 8 Mainreachan Buttress, Fuar Tholl with Rob 16 December 2022

16th December 2022, Mainreachan Buttress on Fuar Tholl

Fuar Tholl hosts a couple of the mythical big crags in the North West which I’d read about in the guidebook for years, but the opportunity to go there had never quite materialised. Towards the end of the long and very cold spell of weather in December, Rob and I made a speculative plan to meet up there, and after finishing work on Thursday I made the long drive from Edinburgh by myself. The thermometer on the car dropped to double digits over Drumochter pass, but my optimism took a hit when Rob reported heavy snow to the road in Glen Carron. I got as far as Garve with everything looking very dry, but with every junction from that point the road was buried deeper and deeper. The only vehicles getting up and down the Glen Carron road were the indefagitable Tesco delivery vans. It has become an ongoing joke seeing these vans charging up and down roads covered in feet of snow in the North West..

After a luxurious night in the van we set off on skis. I put my skis on from the road for the principle, but after 15 minutes the narrow and steep path forced them back off for a while. Higher up, we were treated a lovely pastel dawn as the skiing started in earnest.

Expectations of success were pretty low as we slogged through deep powder but when Mainreachan buttress finally loomed out through the mist it actually looked reassuringly black, and when I got to grips with the first pitch it was even drier than I thought, with the inside of cracks being dry enough for cam placements. This was extra suprising, given that at this point in mid December we were coming towards the end of a lovely two week spell of cold, calm-ish, high pressure northerly weather, with the freezing level remaining very low the whole time. I’m still suprised that the northerlies didn’t rime up the north facing wall. Rime is formed by supercooled water droplets slamming into the crag (unlike hoar frost which forms directly from water vapour freezing onto a surface), so for whatever reason that airflow never ended up carrying supercooled water droplets and instead just dumped snow on the hills. Being suprised by conditions is all part of the fun.. Anyhow, I picked my way up the first pitch, and strung the ledgier ground above into a big rope stretcher to land underneath the obvious crux pitch.

Rob set off up the brilliant initial crack section with intent. We thought the crux move came just at the top of this section, mantelling onto a ledge with some thin hooks. At this point Rob hammered in a bomber lost arrow peg, shortly after I had been complaining that they never go in. I think the justification for Rob’s massive piton rack goes along the lines of it being far more likely to back off a pitch due to a lack of gear, than to fail on a pitch due to being too heavy..

There was plenty more interesting climbing above, with some tenous traverse moves and then a wild section hooking and then sitting on top of a prominent sharp spike. The exit to the right of a roof above was also an immensely satisfying big move. Rob was belayed just below the next major terrace system, and it was really unclear exactly which way to go at this point. By the sounds of things Rob and I climbed something to the left of where Iain and Hugh went a couple of weeks later, so it’s probably all climbable.. There was a big steep barrier wall blocking access to a corner, and on the basis of the route description of a corner which eased with height I forged up a steep wide crack with just enough gear to encourage me onwards, and pulled over a steep section to be rewarded with some brilliant hooks just where you needed them. A really questy bit of climbing, probably worth VII. The upper corner feature was straightforward and led us up to another lovely belay stance, next to this amazing tiny tree hanging on to the crag up around 800m.

Rob charged up a final easy pitch of climbing in the dark, and then we had a fairly straightforward descent around the side in some good (non-avalanchey) snow. The feeling of commitment and isolation high on the top of Fuar Tholl was very good. Although I was very happy to follow Rob and his brilliant OS maps app at the same time. After packing up below the cliffs, I can’t claim that the ski out was the best skiing I’ve ever done, it would probably be more fun with skins on all the way down.. All in all it was a brilliant end to the start of the season, a long awaited ski/climb combination, and a definite lifetime scottish winter route tick.

I then immediately drove all the way back to the Raeburn that evening, and then on Saturday all the way down to Sheffield for a party at which I caught a Norovirus-style vomiting bug in a whisky tasting game.. I’m not suprised I was wiped out for the rest of 2022!
