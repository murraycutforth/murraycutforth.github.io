*20th January 2023, Buachaille Etive Mor*

7AM: Alarms blare in unison throughout the Caol climbing commune. Three fried eggs and we're off.

8:45AM: The i20 rolls into the layby and we walk out, it's a perfect winter day. The path is frozen into an icy runway promising perfect conditions on the hill, it looks like the real deal.

9:30AM: Reality strikes and we realise that most of the Buachaille is rapidly stripping in the blazing sunshine! Not something you normally have to account for. We have a quick brainstorm and casually decide on Raven's Edge. Rob is the only one of us who knows a bit more about the reputation of Raven's Edge, and I sensed a bit of trepidation from him at the time, which makes me laugh now. He was definitely right!

10:30AM: Rob takes the first two pitches, starting up a fairly easy but completely buried spur to the right of Raven's gully. Slow going with all the excavation but so far, so good.

12PM: Rob sets off on the short second pitch which doesn't look like much, probably already looking forward to chilling out on the friendly end of the rope for the rest of the day after his block is over! As he reveals the climbing from underneath a sheet of powder a truly heinous sequence of marginal hooks over marginal gear appears. It says a lot that the best runner was probably the deep powder banking out the gully 4 metres below. It was a real head game. Between the deep snow underneath and the poor gear, the risk calculus is probably in your favour but you would never want to test it. With hindsight, after such a big lead we could never turn around and our benightment was now sealed!

2PM: Our belay efficiency isn't helped by a complete lack of 240cm slings (naming no names) but eventually I'm off into the next pitch which looks fierce. Feeling psyched for it, but excess enthusiasm is soon snuffed out by a bold sequence straight off the belay where I settle into "must not make any mistakes" mode. Annoyingly I then treat the traverse with more respect than it deserves, once the powder has been cleared it's straightforward face moves over to a big spike which marks entry to the next section of climbing. I do find it hard to go back to a more aggressive approach after getting into a defensive mode mid pitch.. Shouldering both axes I then layback a big flake and properly get established into a slightly overhanging corner section. This took a while to figure out but once I cleared the path above, plugged in some gear, and committed to a series of torques with my feet opposing on tiny positive edges I could find the odd niche to stand in balance and the flow started to come back. Or maybe I just started to trust the relentless torque moves. With kit at critically low levels I belay just underneath the top of the corner. It was obviously mid-sunset by then, and my main worry is now that my mammoth lead might have drained Rob and Will's enthusiasm..

5PM: I should have known better, the boys arrive full of psyche and Will sets off into the night for the final three pitches! Our motivation is fuelled by a hat-trick of unsuccessful walk ins the previous weekend, and the impending thaw. The stars appear over Rannoch moor, and it actually feels incredibly relaxing to commit to climbing over the top, however long it takes. At some point, Rob and I spot a helicopter flying across Glen Coe- "I swear that heli is flashing a light at us"… "It's coming right towards us"! "Did a tourist call mountain rescue"?! The S92 thunders overhead and after taking a look at us starts sweeping up and down North Buttress just to our side. It makes a couple of back and forth trips over the next hour, and we later found out that someone had unfortunately been injured over there.

9PM: High on the penultimate pitch, curses and grunts float downwards, signalling that Will has found the offwidth! It felt like the end of the world to follow, with axes, ropes and rucksack all doing their best to stop me going anywhere. Desperate! I clearly learned nothing in Yosemite. I almost forgot to mention another unique section of the route just before the offwidth; a cramped traverse left under a big roof culminating in a section where you crawl around a huge nest. Looking it up now I think it really is a Raven's nest- big, untidy, woven from rough twigs.

~10ish: Although the route was all done we still weren't sure what the best descent option would be, the Buachaille is a complex mountain and we had bags sitting half way up great gully. After some careful investigation of the snow, we end up downclimbing all the way on Great Gully. A lot of wind redistribution has left fairly firm neve down one side of the gully, but I've never been keen on steep snow and the bottom section felt mildly uncomfortable. It was good to catch up with the boys with the last of the technical ground over with!

12:30AM: Back at the car!

1:30AM: We pillage the kitchen with a gourmet combination of scrambled eggs, instant noodles, granola, and left over curry. Saturday was a glorious blur of loafing and eating: first breakfast, Morrison's cafe for second breakfast, lunch, dinner, desert.. And then a little taster of the fort william nightlife but the less said about that the better!
