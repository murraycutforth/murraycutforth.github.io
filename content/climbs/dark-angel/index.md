![The approach to Church Door Buttress](IMG_5987.jpg)

Introducing Michael Chan could fill a library. He's a supremely talented climber and a man who cannot be put into a box. 
We met in Edinburgh back in my early days there. Michael was in a sport climbing incarnation, and I remember him demolishing most of the routes at Upper Cave on our first encounter. While I was in California Michael settled in Inverness. 
We've been out together a fair bit this season, cranking out some amazing routes.

On the day, the usual 5AM alarm, tea, yoghurt and granola saw us out the door and into a typically wet and dark west coast approach. Take your pick from faith, stubborn-ness, or stupidity. When it's dark, wet and windy at the bottom of the glen then at least one of the above is required to get out the car and start walking up the hill. The approach to Church Door is a grind, but eventually we made it through the darkness and the rain to emerge into a winter wonderland. We had a look at New Age Raiders but it seemed to be quite banked out and so we decided on the known quantity of Dark Angel.

This route was put up by Helliker, Bullock, and Whittaker — three professional climbers who are famous enough not to need first names, but who aren't often found scratching around in the highlands. An impressive set of my friends had fallen off the crux second pitch the previous year, including Michael himself who was keen for a rematch. The first pitch is quite a stout VII in itself, but I had a reasonable amount of fore-knowledge (Rob and I made the first summer ascent of this route in 2023 at E1). There's an exciting swing out left to the belay, where it's important to spread the gear out as the blocky features here are not beyond suspicion.

![Michael on the first pitch](IMG_5997.jpg)

Michael took the sharp end, and put on a masterclass of laybacking, torquing, and his signature move- the "chan opener". I'm not going to give everything away here, but Michael has popularised a radical manouevre which involves inverting the ice axe and is a game changer in .5 cracks like this. At this point in time, I had only ever heard about this second hand, and never used it myself. Michael was giving it full beans on the headwall crack on pitch two. At one point, another climber emerged in the gully underneath and stopped, seemingly transfixed by the scene on the wall. Michael avoided the big whip, but only just! And he's normally cool as they come on this grade.

Following this pitch added a new dimension to winter climbing for me. I transferred onto the headwall crack, climbed to the final conventional hook, and there I stayed for the next 10 minutes. My style in winter is slow and controlled. I don't tend to go for low percentage moves or fall off. I could not commit to this tenuous layback. Feeling more bewildered than anything else, I tried the only other thing I could think of. I flipped an axe, slotted it in as high as possible, and cranked it down (do not attempt this with a Petzl Nomic). To my suprise, it felt pretty solid. I flipped the other axe, locked off, and repeated the procedure. The axes are cammed, so conveniently and terrifyingly they just fall out as soon as you unweight them. I climbed most of the crack like this, with both axes upside down. At the same time, my preconceptions about what you can do with an ice axe were also being upended.

I spent the rest of the winter constantly looking for opportunities to deploy this move, and almost every mixed route I did could be made easier and (more importantly) more fun with it.
