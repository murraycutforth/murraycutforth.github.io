*8th March 2023, Am Basteir*

We've reached the penultimate route of last winter now, which occurred on another one of those "best day of the winter" days. This being on Wednesday, I secured yet another last minute day off from my long-suffering manager and plans were set in motion!

Iain and I headed over from the fort, meeting up with Rob at Kyle of Lochalsh where we spent the night in the vans in a secret otter-friendly car park. We could see the hills on Skye gleaming white in the moonlight in the distance. So far so good..

**Diversion into some rambling about the weather at that time:**

It was a relief to see the hills were actually white on Skye because the weather in final week of Feb / first week of March had been really unusual. It all started with a sudden stratospheric warming (SSW) event over the Arctic on the 16th of Feb. Triggered by a phenomenon called Rossby waves, a huge upwelling in the atmosphere resulted in the temperature in the arctic stratosphere (10km – 50km up) shooting up by 50 degrees in the space of a couple of days, and disrupting the polar night jet, which is an extremely strong stratospheric jet stream which spins up every winter due to the temperature difference between the equator and the pole. Normally, the polar night jet flows from west to east (due to the direction of Earth's spin) and helps to strengthen and stabilise the tropospheric jet stream, which influences our weather. When the tropospheric jet is strong and stable, cold arctic air tends to be contained up north, and we tend to get a procession of milder low pressure systems which are drawn in from the atlantic. However, in the weeks after this SSW on the 16th of Feb, the polar night jet broke down, and in turn our local tropospheric jet started to exhibit all the stability of a Tory government. By the end of Feb we ended up with a large high pressure system sitting to the NW, and no jet stream to push it away. This was mostly brilliant, since it drew in freezing cold air from the north while giving week after week of lovely weather, but because it hadn't been preceded by any big winter storms, the mountains were incredibly dry and climbing conditions weren't always amazing, especially out west.

It's a really surprisingly long slog over the moorland to approach Am Basteir, powder-covered heather isn't particularly quick at the best of times. I still couldn't tell what the conditions would be like on the route- surely it would be way too steep to be buried in powder?! As the route finally loomed overhead 14 hours after leaving home, I realised we had won big!

I think it was Rob's turn for a big lead after the gathering, so he racked up underneath the extremely intimidating first pitch. I've never seen anything like it in the context of a winter route, the amount of outward horizontal distance is completely ludicrous. There was a really obvious chockstone to aim for, and then a massive roof section into the final vert wall of the pitch. Rob stripped down to just a base layer and soft shell for the lead, the winter climbing equivalent of going completely topless. I could suddenly understand Iain's story about how Nick Carter, upon making the first winter ascent with Martin Morgan, gave up winter climbing for the rest of the season, and they climbed it in November!

Rob proceeded to put on a masterclass on pitch 1, and absolutely cruised through some very steep moves without even a single power whimper, let alone a power scream. I was facing outward to belay for the first time ever in winter, Iain was snapping away on the camera. It was a good start! The pitch ended in a decent belay niche underneath yet more arm meltingly steep ground.

With a lot more trepidation than Rob I set off on the lead on P2, and got drawn upwards with great hooks and gear. Things came to a head when the groove feature ended, and after scratching around for a while at max extension there was a moment of full commitment to a pretty rubbish hook. After a very very gentle match then we were back to sinker hooks! So good..

<!-- TODO: add photo - "2nd belay views" -->

The third pitch was almost out of sight from the belay ledge, but Rob was on fire today and so we fired him up yet another ludicrously steep chimney groove system. We could watch the ice and snow falling off the route above moving further and further out into space away from us, as he got higher. We thought it might be in the bag, but then the final part of the pitch featured a massive sting in the tail! For me this pitch was easily the crux. On second I was thrutching around on hand jams and crap hooks, I found it to be way more technical than the other pitches. The final steep wall featured a single key chockstone, and Rob eventually did something mad involving a full span ice axe undercling off this to get past. Let us pray that this chockstone never blows!

The final pitch was more traditional, classic off-widthy stuff and I channelled my inner chimney sweep once again before iain guided us down some awkward dark abseils.

In conclusion, this was a genuine 10/10. I was pretty terrified of it, but it wouldn't have been so much fun if I hadn't.
