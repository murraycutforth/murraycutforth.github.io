*18th January 2020, Church Door Buttress, Bidean Nam Bian*

January was a tough month for winter climbing in Scotland. An exceptionally warm Christmas period completely stripped the mountains, and for the rest of the month the weather seemed to alternate between massive storms and deep thaws which stopped any build up. Despite several attempts to get out, this was the only route I managed in the whole month. Luckily it was a belter!

Un Poco Loco takes the face directly underneath the arch on church door buttress. There are several variations on the line, but the direct start and direct finish are the way to go. Without a guidebook description for the direct finish, all I knew was that pitch ended up directly underneath, and then outrageously threads a hole behind the huge rock arch capping the face.

Me and Will had endlessly talked up this route from the safety of facebook messenger and had got as far as walking in to try it earlier in the season. But this time, with the approach frozen underfoot from the valley, it was clearly on. A rather poorly Iain had also been infected by the hype last minute and joined us, coughing up some psychedelic-looking mucus as we walked in. Reaching the crag base, we had hit the jackpot with conditions. Heavily frozen, but just lightly rimed up. There had been heavy snow, but the route is way too steep to hold much powder. Right in the middle of the sweet spot for steep hooky mixed climbing.

Will bravely jumped on the first pitch despite us knowing from UKC that this featured a so-called death flake half way up. After a few boulder problems between ledges it became obvious what the issue was. A huge flake has detached from the rest of the cliff and fallen sideways across a groove half way up the pitch. Unfortunately this groove is the way up for the route, and the flake does a good job of blocking it. Me and Iain prepared to dive out the way as Will hooked the back of the flake, eventually fully yarding off it and standing on the top. It bizarrely feels fairly stable, and there's no movement in it, but it looks ripe. More drama was yet to come, and as Will committed to the crux of the pitch up a wide crack he slammed in a boot jam with the full weight of his Nepal Extremes. After a minute of desperate wiggling, the boot came out but with his crampon loosely dangling off the ankle strap! Will resisted the urge to add to his collection of whippers and luckily could place gear where he was to hang off and get sorted out. After watching all this, both me and Iain proceeded to get our boot stuck in the exact same spot when seconding.

At this point, Iain's illness had shockingly not improved from standing still in a blizzard for an hour or two, and he abseiled off from the belay rather than perch on a tiny ledge for a few more hours. My turn for a big lead!

The direct finish was everything I dream about in Scottish winter. There's no easy ground, but initially the moves are relatively secure. After stepping off the top of a column, things got progressively thinner and thinner. I ground to a halt near the top of the corner system. Three times I locked off my top axe and groped blindly around the groove above for the next hook, and every time I came out empty handed with my left bicep on fire. It was taking longer and longer to recover on the marginal rest below this point, and I felt like there were only a few attempts left. Working my feet up as high as I could get them, this time the axe dropped into a sinker hook in a completely unexpected position. I matched the axe and spanned out at full stretch to the right, just able to find a hook in the back of a parallel corner system. As if by design, there's a single tiny slot on the blank slab between the two corner systems at foot height. Standing up into this leaves you in the crucifix position, spread across the rock between the two corners. Heart in mouth, I dynamically swapped the right front point for the left front point in the tiny foot slot, and then dropped down onto the right-hand axe, safely in the final corner system leading behind the arch.

The arch. Thousands of tons of rock held in place solely by the geometry of some ancient rockfall, hanging over the face. Back-and-footing up the hanging chimney behind it, I was trying not to push outwards too hard! One final squirm and I popped out onto the big ledge on top, where Ed and Felicity were engaged with Raeburn's chimney (it's deceptively desperate). Sweaty and content, I chatted to Ed as Will started up the pitch. As per usual, during the big lead I had entered a parallel space-time dimension where time passes at a massively accelerated rate. What had felt like minutes in my frame was actually almost two and a half hours.

We took the easy option at this point and abbed off from the in-situ above the arch, not wanting to deal with the scrappier exit pitches above. The abseil from the arch is world class as abseils go- after an awkward slither off the ledge I remember spinning slowly in space, with the crag totally silent and large feathery snowflakes falling past the beam of my headtorch into the dusk below. It wasn't until around the first belay that I hung within touching distance of the face again. A social evening in Craigallan with a bunch of friends from CUMC was the icing on the cake of a great day out.

## P.S.

At the same time as all this, one of the most heroic episodes in Scottish winter climbing history had been going on just around the corner on the same cliff. Relying only on his extremely strongly-held belief that winter climbing is just not that hard, Luke had jumped on a niche VII 7 called critical mass, rarely (if ever) climbed since the first ascent. With infinite patience his brother Paul belayed him on a mammoth four hour lead as he forced his way up the first (and probably the crux) pitch. After their late start driving up from Edinburgh the same morning they decided to call it a day, but a legendary effort nonetheless for his second Scottish winter route ever. There's a lot that being a trad weapon can't compensate for, but winter climbing is apparently not one of them.
